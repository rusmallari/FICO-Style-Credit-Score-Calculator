How to fill this out
01
On-time payments
Count how many individual bill payments you made on time across all your accounts in the past 12 months. This includes credit cards, auto loans, student loans, and any other lines of credit. If you have 3 credit cards and paid each one on time every month, that's 36 on-time payments. Even one missed payment can significantly drag down your score — this factor alone accounts for 35% of your total.
02
Total payments scheduled
This is the total number of payments that were due across all your accounts over the past 12 months — whether you paid them or not. Using the same example above, if you had 3 credit cards with monthly due dates, your total scheduled payments would be 36. Dividing on-time payments by this number gives your payment history percentage, the single biggest factor in your score.
03
Total credit limit
Add up the credit limits from all your revolving credit accounts — primarily credit cards and lines of credit. For example, if you have one card with a $5,000 limit and another with a $3,000 limit, your total credit limit is $8,000. Do not include installment loans like car loans or mortgages, as those have fixed payoff schedules and are treated differently by FICO.
04
Credit used
Enter the total outstanding balances you currently carry across all your revolving accounts. If your two credit cards have balances of $1,200 and $800, enter $2,000. This number divided by your total credit limit gives your utilization ratio. Lenders like to see this below 30% — the lower the better. High utilization signals financial stress even if you always pay on time.
05
Credit history length
Enter the average age of all your open credit accounts in years. If your oldest card is 8 years old and your newest is 2 years old, a rough average would be around 5 years. A longer credit history generally helps your score because it gives lenders more data to assess your reliability. This is why it's usually a bad idea to close old accounts even if you don't use them — it shortens your average history.
06
Recent inquiries
A hard inquiry occurs when a lender checks your credit as part of a loan or card application. Enter how many times this has happened in the past 12 months. Checking your own credit score does not count — that's a soft inquiry and has no effect. Multiple hard inquiries in a short window can suggest you're taking on more debt than you can handle, so applying for several new accounts at once can hurt your score.

Common questions
Your credit score is one of the most influential numbers in your financial life. Lenders use it to decide whether to approve you for credit cards, car loans, mortgages, and more — and at what interest rate. A higher score means lower rates, which can save you thousands of dollars over the life of a loan. Landlords, insurers, and even some employers check it too.
A strong score unlocks better financial options — lower APRs on credit cards, easier apartment approvals, cheaper car insurance premiums, and larger loan amounts. A weak score can mean higher deposits, loan denials, or being stuck paying significantly more interest over time. Even a 50-point difference can change the rate you're offered on a mortgage by half a percent or more.
The two highest-impact moves are paying every bill on time and reducing your credit utilization below 30% (ideally below 10%). Payment history accounts for 35% of your score, and utilization 30% — together they drive 65% of the total. Setting up autopay for at least the minimum payment eliminates late payments entirely. Paying down a high credit card balance can raise your score within a single billing cycle.
Credit utilization is the percentage of your available revolving credit that you're currently using. If you have a $10,000 limit and carry a $3,000 balance, your utilization is 30%. Lenders see high utilization as a sign of financial strain. Keeping it under 30% is good; under 10% is excellent. It's also one of the fastest factors to change — paying down a balance improves it immediately.
